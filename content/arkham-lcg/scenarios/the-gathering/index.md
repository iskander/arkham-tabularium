## Overview

The Gathering drops you into Roland Banks' or Wendy Adams' Arkham home as something has gone wrong with the world. Monsters are appearing. You need to investigate, seal the gate, and survive. It's a compact, elegant scenario that teaches you the game's core loops without ever feeling like a tutorial.

That's its greatest trick: it *is* a tutorial, but it doesn't feel like one. The stakes feel real from the first chaos token pull.

## Encounter deck composition

The encounter deck is deliberately forgiving — cultists, ghouls, geists — but the density of enemy cards is high enough to create pressure. The ghoul priest lurks in there as a potential boss, and his first appearance can end runs abruptly if you've over-committed to clue-gathering.

<div class="callout note">
  <p class="callout-title">Note</p>
  <p>On Standard, most players will see the Ghoul Priest before the third agenda. On Hard/Expert, the first two act stages demand real resource discipline.</p>
</div>

## Narrative & theme

The slow creep of wrongness in your own home is captured well. The location flavour text earns its keep. The agenda sequence — from unease to full-blown horror — is paced almost perfectly for a first experience.

What holds it back slightly: the resolution options are thin. Your choices feel predetermined by how the encounter deck treated you, not by genuine agency.

## Mechanical design

The three-location structure (Study, Hallway, Attic/Cellar) creates a natural tension between clue-hunting and threat management. You're constantly making the same real decision: push toward the clues or deal with the ghoul that just spawned?

The resign option is often underrated here. Knowing when to bail is the first skill AH:LCG teaches, and The Gathering is patient enough to let players learn it without punishing them for it.

## Verdict

The best first scenario in any LCG I've played. Not the most complex, not the most memorable, but structurally close to perfect for what it needs to do.
