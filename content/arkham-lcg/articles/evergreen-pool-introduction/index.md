Chapter 2 of Arkham Horror: The Card Game has fully landed. The Core Set and all five evergreen decks are out, and we now have a complete, self-contained starting environment — what is now called the Chapter 2 _Current_ card pool. Five investigators, five evergreen decks, a reshaped foundation on top of which every expansion in this chapter will build.

Before new content starts arriving and starts shaping Chapter 2, it's worth stepping back and looking at this pool on its own terms. The designers stated that Chapter 2 was the chance to rebalance and reposition the identities of each class, so we can now evaluate the pool against those design goals.

## What the Series Does

Each installment takes one class — **Guardian**, **Seeker**, **Rogue**, **Mystic**, **Survivor** — and runs it through the same lens:

- **What the manual says.** How the Chapter 2 Core rulebook characterizes the class: its stated strengths, its named playstyles, the identity it claims.
- **How the Core Set implements it.** The Core investigator, their signature card and weakness, and the shared class pool.
- **How the Evergreen deck expands it.** The second investigator, their kit, and the cards added by their deck.
- **Named playstyles, checked against reality.** Are the four playstyles the manual promises actually realized? Which ones are well-founded, which are nascent, and which are more aspirational than built?
- **Implicit archetypes and threads.** The patterns that emerge across the pool that the manual _doesn't_ name — the subthemes, the mechanical identities hiding in plain sight.
- **Conclusions.** Where the class stands as a whole, and what the design space looks like going forward.

The goal is to get a clear read on how each class is defined and delivered, so that when new expansions arrive, you have a baseline to see what's being reinforced, what's being patched, and what's being taken somewhere new.

## What the Series Doesn't Do

A few things worth being upfront about.

**This is not a power-level review.** The cards have just come out. I have not had enough table time with them to grade them with any rigor. Where a card's mechanical role is clear, it gets discussed; where its value is going to depend on play experience, the series stays out of it.

**This is not a Chapter 1 comparison.** The Chapter 2evergreen pool is evaluated as an ensemble, on its own terms. Chapter 1 is occasionally referenced where a design choice only makes sense against that history — Rogue pivoting away from guns, for instance — but the point isn't "better or worse than Dunwich-era Seeker." It's "what does this class look like, as the foundation of Chapter 2?"

**This is not a deck-building guide.** There are people doing excellent work on that front, and the series links to them when appropriate.

## Why Read Each Part

Each class has its own story, and each installment tries to surface what's most interesting about that particular design.

- **Guardian** is the cleanest test case for the manual-vs-pool framework, with three of four playstyles well-supported and a fourth (Medic) that quietly drifted to other classes. It's also where the unnamed _Police_ subtheme shows up as a whole identity the manual doesn't acknowledge.
- **Seeker** is where the evergreen pool introduces the most mechanically interesting new theme in the chapter — horror as an active resource economy — and where the split between Joe and Carolyn sketches two genuinely distinct class faces.
- **Rogue** is the most _operational_ class description of the five, and the spotlight unpacks what that means for how the playstyles work, why guns were removed, and which seeds are planted for future expansions.
- **Mystic** is the most design-ambitious of the five, with Blood Magic as a genuinely new playstyle, and the clearest gap between a named playstyle (Doombringer) and what the pool actually delivers.
- **Survivor** arrives with the boldest premise — the nascent _Trapper_ archetype, which introduces a new strategic dimension tied to the board itself — and the most uneven delivery across its named playstyles.

If you're wondering which order to read them in: the Guardian post sets up the framework most explicitly, so it's a natural starting point. After that, they stand on their own.

## About Arkham Tabularium

This series runs on **Arkham Tabularium**, a blog which aims to cover games set in the Arkham universe — the Arkham Files line (Arkham Horror: The Card Game, Arkham Horror 3rd edition, Arkham Horror RPG, Elder Sign) and the Call of Cthulhu RPG alongside it. The remit is broad on purpose: the same mythos keeps being re-interpreted across very different design traditions, and those interpretations illuminate each other. Card game analysis is one thread among several.
